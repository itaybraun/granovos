# True Kindness – Narrative Synopsis

The graphic novel **"A True Kindness"** is a powerful and harrowing story that weaves together questions of faith, morality, and the psychological toll of war amidst the chaos of the **1973 Yom Kippur War**. The novel is based on a true story.

The title refers to a profound Jewish concept regarding the selfless act of caring for the dead (*Chesed Shel Emet*). In Jewish tradition, this is considered the purest form of kindness because the deceased can never return the favor. However, the story explores a deeper, more haunting meaning: a "true kindness" that has nothing to do with the truth, where a merciful lie becomes the ultimate sacrifice for the sake of the living.

---

## Project Details
* **Genre:** Historical Drama / Graphic Novel
* **Period:** The Yom Kippur War (1973) and its aftermath (1974)
* **Length:** Approx. 110 pages

---

## Plot Summary

### Prologue: Shards
**November 1974.** Sergeant Gadi Ashkenazi returns from Egyptian captivity. Like other POWs, he undergoes humiliating interrogations at a military facility in Zichron Ya'akov. This time, the interrogation takes a different turn with the arrival of **Major Singer**, commander of the Southern Command’s Burial Unit. Gadi provides testimony regarding desperate combat, the abandonment of an outpost in the dead of night, a navigational error, and his subsequent capture. He offers the sole evidence regarding the fate of the soldiers who did not survive that day.

### Part I: In the Sinai Desert
**October 7, 1973.** On the second day of the war, two kosher supervisors, **Miklosh and Aharon**, are drafted into the Burial Unit and sent into the heart of the Sinai inferno under the command of Major Singer and his tough deputy, Menachem. The first encounter with the bodies of both enemies and brothers-in-arms shatters the soldiers, especially Miklosh, a quiet and sensitive man.

Late at night, Miklosh decides to desert. He fears prison and social humiliation, but he fears even more that his soul will remain permanently scarred. An encounter with another soldier attempting to flee leads Miklosh to a realization: their task is to remember that every fallen soldier was once "the most beautiful thing in the world" to his family. He decides to stay. Moving through the battlefields of the "Chinese Farm" under heavy artillery fire, Miklosh discovers the personal diary of a missing soldier, **Yoav Sonnenschein**, and keeps it a secret, defying orders to bury blood-soaked items.

### Part II: True Kindness
**January 1974.** Due to the staggering number of unidentified soldiers, they are transferred to a temporary cemetery in **Be'eri**. The encounter with bereaved families weighs heavily on Miklosh. A bereaved brother begs him to lie to his mother about the exact time of her son's death; the official report contradicts the moment she "felt" him leave the world, destroying her peace. The brother argues that lying to provide comfort is the ultimate *Chesed Shel Emet*.

The climax occurs when five unidentified bodies remain. Major Singer hints to his men to act "as we did in '48." The soldiers perform the **"Goral HaGra" (The Fate of the Vilna Gaon)**: opening a Bible to find a divine hint to a deceased's identity. Relying on a miracle rather than science, they assign names to the missing, including Yoav Sonnenschein. Miklosh and Aharon are left with a heavy burden: the knowledge that they determined, perhaps erroneously, the name under which a man will be buried forever.

### Part III: Eternal Rest
**June 1974.** The Be'eri cemetery is emptied. At a mass funeral, the soldiers meet Yoav Sonnenschein’s sister. She thanks them for the body and the diary but reveals details about her brother's final moments that contradict their identification. It becomes clear: the identification was an error. However, Miklosh chooses to remain silent. This is his "True Kindness"—a silence meant to prevent further suffering for the living.

### Epilogue
**February 17, 2013 (7th of Adar).** Forty years after the war, Aharon and Miklosh attend a Military Rabbinate event. While Aharon speaks of heroism, Miklosh reveals the **PTSD** that has haunted him: every night, he is back in the smoke of the desert, straightening blankets to preserve the dignity of faces he cannot bear to see. He implores younger soldiers to seek help. 

Aharon admits he feared Miklosh would reveal the truth about the *Goral HaGra*. Miklosh replies that, like Moses, there are soldiers whose exact burial place is unknown to this day—and it is still too early to speak of it.
