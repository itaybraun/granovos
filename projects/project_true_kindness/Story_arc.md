

# Story Arc — A True act of Kindness (Chesed Shel Emet)

## Prologue — Shards

**November 1974. Two months after the Yom Kippur War.**

At a Military Intelligence interrogation facility in Zichron Ya'akov, returning POWs are compelled to relive their capture again and again. The atmosphere is one of humiliation and accusation: real soldiers kill or die — they don't get taken prisoner.

Sergeant G.A. refuses to cooperate. He has already told his story; he sees no reason to repeat it for interrogators who will only use it to shame him. When the interrogator mocks his resistance, he reveals that a senior Rabbinical Corps officer is also present — not to interrogate, but to collect testimony needed to declare deaths and release agunot (women legally bound to missing husbands) under Jewish law.

**Major Zinger** introduces himself: commander of the burial unit of Southern Command. He opens a large black binder marked *IDF Southern Command — Chesed Shel Emet* ("True Kindness," the traditional Hebrew term for care of the dead). He says simply: "I'm listening."

G.A. describes the retreat from the fortified post "Milano" on the bank of the Suez Canal — the lies, the broken promises of rescue, the impossible choices left to the soldiers themselves. A navigation error led his group into the abandoned city of Kantara, where they were captured by morning without a fight. He describes the terror, the helplessness, the two men who wanted to take their own lives — and how he talked them out of it. Now the survivors are angry at him, and the interrogators are angry that he survived.

Major Zinger, who has been silent the whole time, responds with a single line: *"Whoever saves one soul of Israel is as if he saved an entire world."* The interrogating officer shoots him a sharp look.

G.A. names the fallen, describes how they died, and where they remain buried in Kantara. He closes with gratitude to the rabbi: *"You have my testimony. Now go and bring them home. What you go through is no less difficult than what we went through. I wouldn't be in a hurry to trade places with you."*

## Part I — In the Sinai Desert

### Faithful of Spirit *(Neeman Ruach)*

**Day two of the war.** Miklos and Aharon — two kosher-supervision clerks — are summoned urgently to the base chaplain. Aharon jokes; Miklos already knows what it means.

In the office, two officers are waiting. On the table: a thick black binder labeled *Chesed Shel Emet*. Major Zinger asks only one question: can Miklos keep a secret? The base officer quotes Proverbs — *"A faithful spirit conceals a matter."* Miklos stands throughout the interview without saying a word. He exits stooped. Aharon goes in next.

### The Most Beautiful Thing in the World

The unit arrives at Rephidim Base in Sinai. Major Zinger delivers a long, pathos-filled speech — scripture, the valor of the Jewish people, the complexity of their mission, a reminder to respect the dead including Egyptians, a hint not to loot, and an open invitation to anyone who cannot carry this burden to come speak with him privately.

Aharon is moved. Then they go outside where several Egyptian bodies lie in poor condition. Captain Menachem, Zinger's second-in-command, threatens the soldier who breaks down and begs to be released from the unit. He must return to his post or face a court-martial for mutiny. Through tears, he goes back.

Aharon whispers to Miklos: *"I was wrong. This is all theater. The kind manager plays the compassionate man, and beside him there's an attack dog making sure the work gets done."*

That night, Miklos decides to flee to a nearby base and blend in with kitchen workers until the war ends. In the dark, he runs into the soldier who was refused release — also trying to escape. They walk together in silence. The soldier says the prison sentence will be temporary, but serving in a burial unit will damage his soul permanently. Miklos tries to reassure him, and in doing so finds words he didn't know he had:

> *"Those soldiers we'll have to handle tomorrow — for twenty years, maybe more, they were the most beautiful thing in the world to their parents, their friends, their children. And now, for just a few days, they look terrible. We have to remember and honor who they once were."*

*"It's not the same thing,"* says the other soldier. *"It is the same thing,"* Miklos answers. He turns back to base.

Aharon has been looking for him everywhere. When Miklos admits he considered deserting, Aharon doesn't scold him: *"Don't lie to me. If you don't want to say it, don't say it. Let's sleep — tomorrow is a hard day."*

### *Ribon Shel Olam* (Lord of the Universe)

*Homage to the song by the late Captain Barry Hazak.*

### The Valley of the Shadow *(Tzalmavet)*

Morning. The unit drives to a battlefield near the Canal for their first identification assignment. Sitting in the vehicle, no one moves until the officer says: *"All right. Let's get this done."* Miklos asks for five more minutes and stares at the sky. Aharon sprints back to the truck and vomits.

On the festival of Sukkot, part of the unit refuses to drive — the holiness of the holiday forbids it. Major Zinger rebukes them sharply: the Chief Military Rabbi has ruled that leaving the fallen in the field would damage the morale of their still-fighting comrades, constituting a *pikuach nefesh* (life-threatening situation) that overrides the Sabbath prohibition. They drive.

On a later mission, riding in an APC close to the front, the convoy nearly takes a missile hit. A tank unit nearby is in combat; one tank is struck and bursts into flames. A wounded man in their vehicle demands they stop and help. The burial unit refuses.

*"We're not heroes,"* Miklos explains. *"No one can replace us. There are thousands of tank soldiers, but how many people are capable of collecting the dead? They barely found six of us who agreed."*

### On October 19th — the Chinese Farm *(HaChava HaSinit)*

The unit reaches the area called "the Chinese Farm," site of three days of catastrophic fighting. The briefing is grim: over 150 killed, an unknown number of missing — presumably buried somewhere in the sand.

During the search, Miklos finds a blood-soaked notebook belonging to Sergeant Yoav Zonnenschein, titled *Combat Journal*. Major Zinger orders it buried — there is blood on it. Miklos protests: the family would surely want it. Zinger insists. Miklos slips the notebook into his bag.

That night he pages through it. Aharon asks about the author. Miklos says he's one of the missing — they'll send it to his family in a day or two, once they find him.

### Only the Dead See the End of War

Near the Matzmed compound, IDF forces have crossed the Canal. The burial unit makes repeated runs into the compound under artillery fire to retrieve the fallen.

Aharon enters the tent with news: the ceasefire this time seems real. The war is almost over. Cheers — then silence as he adds: *"Unlike the fighters, who'll be home in a few weeks, the Rabbinical Corps is expected to stay in the field for a full year. Searches for the missing, identifications, funerals. Over a thousand fallen on the southern front alone."*

The soldiers refuse to believe it.

*"How many casualties?"* the operations sergeant asks over the radio. *"How many — eighty? Eight times ten? Did I hear right? Over."*

### What Shall We Call It?

An extended interlude on the naming of wars. *"War of Liberation"? "War of Independence"?* Neither fits. The Six-Day War suggests its successor should be called "the Eighteen-Day War." The Egyptians call it the October War or the Ramadan War. Major Zinger prefers "the Day of Judgment War." The press calls it "the Yom Kippur War" — strange, since it lasted twenty days and wasn't named for its duration.

Some soldiers call it simply "the War of the Failure."

Miklos doesn't much care what it's called. A few days ago Yehoram Gaon came to the base and sang a new song: *"I promise you, my little girl, this will be the last war."* But Miklos is not a little girl, and he's seen enough now to doubt that promise. There will probably be more wars. He hopes at least the next one gets a better name — one that inspires hope, unifies the people, justifies its own existence.

## Part II — Chesed Shel Emet

### True Kindness *(January 1974)*

Given the staggering number of casualties — approximately 1,400 on the southern front, another 1,000 in the north — a temporary military cemetery is established near Kibbutz Be'eri in the south. Families begin arriving to visit the graves of their sons before the final burials.

The encounters with grieving civilians are crushing. Miklos retreats to his tent and only emerges for the bathroom.

One day a civilian stops him on the path and begs for help. His younger brother was killed on October 8th. Two soldiers who were there say it happened around 11:15 in the morning. But their mother felt a sudden, deep anguish at 5:30 in the afternoon — and a glass vase in the living room fell and shattered without cause. She is certain that was the true moment of her son's death, and she is devastated to learn she was off by hours. The brother asks Miklos to simply confirm in the records that the death was at 5:30. Just a small kindness. A *chesed shel emet* in the truest sense — a lie in service of compassion.

Miklos refuses. The man grabs his arm. Offers money. Offers to bring a great rabbi who will declare it permitted.

*"Let go of me,"* says Miklos. He calls to Aharon from across the field, points toward the man, and retreats to his tent until nightfall.

That night around the fire, Aharon casually mentions he helped a wealthy contractor with just such a request — and that the man donated enough for a mountain of cigarettes. A soldier turns to Miklos: *"I heard he asked you first and you refused. What happened? It's a white lie. Can't you make a grieving mother a little less miserable?"*

Miklos waves it off: *"Of course one can lie for that reason — God himself told Abraham a falsehood to keep the peace at home. My reason is completely different. I work only with the dead, not with their families. I can't. I'm already walking on the edge of an abyss here, barely keeping my balance. I have nothing left beyond what our unit requires of me."*

### Goldilocks and the Three Donkeys *(Zahava VeShlosheth HaChamorim)*

*Background: February 1974. Motti Ashkenazi, commander of the Budapest outpost, demonstrates outside the Knesset. "I didn't surrender to the Egyptians then and I won't surrender today. There was a failure and those responsible must go home. Now."*

An evening in Be'eri. Miklos is leafing through Zonnenschein's notebook; Aharon and two other soldiers join him. Aharon describes the day's success: a soldier was identified by a laundry number sewn into his shirt — a kibbutz system where every member has a unique number on every garment. Someone just had to call every kibbutz in the country to find a match.

Aharon asks what Miklos is looking for in the notebook. Miklos explains that Yoav Zonnenschein is still among the missing — he hoped for a clue, but found only complaints about equipment, battle descriptions, and funny stories. He'll send it to the family at week's end.

Aharon asks for a funny story. Miklos reads aloud *"Goldilocks and the Three Donkeys."* In this version, the three bears are replaced by three donkeys who serve as stand-ins for the wartime government leadership — one who sees catastrophe everywhere, one who wants to break bones, and one who sees no problem whatsoever, everything looks fine. Goldilocks runs away: *"They're not nice."*

Aharon takes the notebook and reads passages Yoav wrote sincerely: *"I will not stay silent, for as long as I can, I will fight for what matters — on the battlefield and after it."* And: *"Don't stop smiling. In all the great difficulty there is still hope."*

*"Are you listening?"* Aharon asks Miklos.

Miklos says yes, he's been listening — mostly to the dead, and they don't say anything. Nothing will change. Will people be more patient, more generous, after the worst blow since the Holocaust? Will the contractor stop cheating his clients? *"In Hungarian we say: when snow falls in red."*

### Many Mercies of Heaven *(Harbeh Chasde Shamayim)*

*Background: Passover 1974 approaches. The temporary cemetery will soon be emptied; mass funerals will be held across the country. But five soldiers in Be'eri remain unidentified.*

Senior logistics officers visit Be'eri to review progress. The commanding officer asks how they managed to identify nearly everyone despite the chaos of war. Major Zinger responds: *"The soldiers did holy work, and there were many mercies of heaven."* The officer keeps pressing. Another senior officer pulls him aside and says in Yiddish: *"If you ask too many questions, you're no longer a wise man. Don't ask hard questions and he won't have to lie. He's religious — he can't lie."*

Major Zinger assembles the unit. Captain Menachem berates them for failing to identify the five unknowns; there will be no Shabbat leave, no departures from base. Then Zinger tells them he met with a great rabbi — whose name he cannot share — who wept and asked only one question: *"Why was Jerusalem destroyed?"*

Outside, the soldiers grumble. Aharon, drawing on years in yeshiva, decodes the message: when a commanding rabbi starts telling convoluted stories about other rabbis who burst into tears, it means he needs you to do something he cannot explicitly order. A kind of *Shabbos goy* arrangement — the instruction must come by implication only. Jerusalem was destroyed because of baseless hatred and idol worship — but they can't figure out how that applies here.

Two days later, no progress. Zinger convenes them again. Aharon bets a pack of cigarettes that there will be another story about a great rabbi who cried. He wins.

Zinger's story: a contractor — a simple, philanthropic Jewish man — visited a Kabbalistic rabbi for guidance on identifying the unknowns. The rabbi said: *"You must do what we did in '48."* And wept. The contractor pressed: what did we do in '48? The rabbi said only: *"The hint is the hint."*

### What We Did in '48 *(Mah She'asinu b'48)*

The next evening, a soldier named Yechezkel bursts in: *"I found it. I found it."*

He had wandered into the kibbutz wheat fields and met a former yeshiva student who had left observance after his brother's death — but retained a phenomenal memory for Talmud. Asked why Jerusalem was destroyed, the man offered a new answer: *"Jerusalem was destroyed because they insisted on the letter of the law and would not go beyond it — would not act before the line of the law."*

Yechezkel explains: the destruction came because people were too rigid, too literal — when they needed to, they could have found a creative, humane interpretation. They just had to want to.

The man then asked what Yechezkel was doing in the army. When he heard *burial unit*, he told him his own brother had died in 1948 as part of the Lamed-Heh convoy. Months after the battle, twelve bodies could not be identified. Rather than a mass grave, they used the *Goral HaGra* — the Vilna Gaon's lottery. An officer stood beside each coffin and opened the Bible at random. The final verse on the last page pointed clearly, in each case, to who lay inside. For his bereaved parents, it brought great comfort.

Yechezkel stopped mid-sentence and ran.

### *Et La'asot L'Hashem, Heferu Toratecha* (Time to Act for God, They Have Violated Your Torah)

The unit is divided. Some refuse to participate in the ritual. One soldier agrees only if a Kabbalistic rabbi conducts it. Aharon refuses: *"Every Jew can perform miracles. As the Rabbi of Kotzk said: Where is God found? Wherever you let Him in."*

Miklos offers a darker reading: Zinger clearly knows what needs to be done but will not say it explicitly — just as the soldiers at the canal were never given explicit orders to retreat. They carry the weight of the decision themselves.

They agree to spend three more days reviewing testimonies. Nothing new emerges. The time has come.

In the small plot of unknowns — five graves. Aharon stands beside the first. Miklos holds five cards bearing the names of those still missing from the Chinese Farm. The first verse that comes reads: *"And the righteousness of our God is like the light of the sun upon our heads."* No obvious connection to any name. 

*"I told you this wouldn't work,"* says the skeptical soldier. *"Quiet — let me think,"* says Aharon.

He flips to another page: *"The sun also rises, and the sun goes down, and hastens to its place where it arose."* Aharon begins to tremble. *"The Shekhinah is with us. We have received the answer and we don't yet understand it."*

After a long silence, someone asks: *"Zonnenschein — that's an Ashkenazi name. What does it mean in Hebrew?"*

Aharon's whole body shakes: *"Zonnenschein in Yiddish means sunlight — sunrise."*

He moves to the next coffin: *"And Moses said to Hobab son of Reuel the Midianite, Moses' father-in-law: We are journeying to the place of which God said, I will give it to you — come with us and we will do good to you."* Miklos places the name **Moshe Yehuda Gutman** on the coffin.

And so, one by one, the five are named.

### Rejoice in Trembling *(Gilu Bir'adah)*

Days later, Yechezkel summons Miklos and Aharon. He has not slept properly since the lottery. Every night he dreams he is pushing an enormous boulder up a hill, and it rolls back down and crushes him.

Miklos reaches for something. He invokes the words of *"Rabbi Camus of Algiers"* — the philosopher's meditation on Sisyphus reframed as a religious teaching. There are two ways to serve God: *"Serve the Lord with joy"* — and *"Serve the Lord with awe, and rejoice in trembling."* How can one both rejoice and tremble? The answer is that true service is complex. Mostly it is done in joy; but there is also awe — the weight of the stone God chooses you to carry. From within that fear, from within that trembling, you are meant to find joy. Joy that you have the inner strength to face it. When the stone rolls back to the bottom and you know you must rise again tomorrow — that moment is *Gilu bir'adah*. The highest form of "always being in joy."

*"People like that,"* Miklos says, *"we are obligated to imagine as happy."*

## Part III — Eternal Rest *(Menuhat Olamim)*

### June 1974 — The Wheat Grows Again

*Background: After Passover, the mass funerals begin. A long convoy of military vehicles departs simultaneously from south and north, spreading out to cities, towns, moshavim, and kibbutzim. The temporary cemetery empties.*

*El male rachamim.* The honor guard presents arms. The national anthem plays. The ceremony ends.

The unit attends a concentrated funeral at a kibbutz. The cantor sings *Unetaneh Tokef* and not an eye stays dry. Afterward, sitting on the grass near the dining hall, a young woman approaches. Her brother is Yoav Zonnenschein. She has come to thank them personally for finding the notebook — the family treasures it. His friends told them about the combat journal and the funny stories he wrote. She adds that he has been nominated for a commendation for bravery: he tried to save tank crew members from a burning tank — and was killed doing it.

The soldiers understand immediately. There has been a terrible mistake in the identification.

The sister sees something is wrong and says: *"It's all right. You don't have to lie. I know."* When Aharon pushes back — *"What do you mean, why would you say that?"* — she reassures them: *"You're so kind-hearted, and so religious, and so bad at lying that it's obvious. I know there's nothing left of him. I know the coffin is mostly earth or bricks. It's all right."*

Aharon steers the conversation: *"Tell us about him. How he lived — not how he died."*

She describes a gentle, universally beloved boy with artistic inclinations. Then, because she senses they're waiting for it, she tells them about the Chinese Farm: Yoav's unit was near a tank that caught fire. He ran to it and helped the driver escape. He climbed back up to save another soldier — and the tank exploded, killing him. The surviving witness was badly wounded in the head, unconscious for two months, and could only recount the story much later after great effort through uncontrollable tears.

The soldiers step away and argue. Aharon wants to inform Major Zinger. Miklos is adamant: *"This isn't a lie — it's chesed shel emet. We cannot cause this family more grief."*

The sister watches them argue from a distance. She turns to a small boy with a dog: *"Go stand near them and pretend to play. Come tell me what they said."*

*And into the evening the valley gathered its beauty, unlike any that had come before.*

## Epilogue

Every year on the seventh of Adar — the traditional anniversary of Moses' death, observed as the day of the Military Rabbinate — Miklos receives an invitation. He has declined thirty-nine times.

**Sign: "Military Rabbinate Day, marking 40 years since the Yom Kippur War. 7 Adar 5773 (February 17, 2013)."**

The emcee calls up the soldiers of the Southern Command burial unit — people who served in the most terrible role for almost a full year, through whose hands 1,400 fallen passed, every one of them a world entire.

**Aharon** speaks: *"The work was very hard. Not suited for everyone. What we did was not simple. We were determined not to leave a single unknown. And we needed many mercies of heaven."*

**Another soldier:** *"My peak moment was when we went to Kantara and brought back the fallen who had been killed in cold blood by the enemy. Blessed that I merited to help bring them to a Jewish grave, and a measure of comfort to their families."*

**Miklos** speaks last: *"Everyone told stories about the war. So allow me to tell about the time after it — when the memories strike without warning. You have children and you can't pull a blanket over them, because suddenly, in one moment, you're standing in a child's room holding a blanket but there are no walls around you. You're standing — with your children — in the yard at Matzmed. Your mouth is full of sand. Around you: smoke, and the artillery deafening your ears. And between all the sand and the bodies and the explosions, a small child is sleeping. The most beautiful thing in the world.*

*Take care of yourselves. Don't suffer alone."*

Aharon and Miklos stand to the side, eating börek. Aharon says: *"That was beautiful."* Miklos answers: *"I planned to say something completely different. I was going to say I'm glad to be here today and that I'm at peace with everything we did, and explain what we did. I was going to tell the audience that today — the anniversary of Moses' death — carries enormous symbolic weight. Because just like Moses, there are soldiers who died in the Sinai desert and whose exact burial place remains unknown to this day."*

*"Too soon to talk about that,"* says Aharon.

*"You're right,"* says Miklos.

Then he adds: *"Let's get out of here. I can't breathe."*
