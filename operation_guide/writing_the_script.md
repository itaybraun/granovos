# How to Write a Graphic Novel Script

A graphic novel script is not a prose story — it is a **blueprint for collaboration**. Your job as the writer is to communicate clearly with an artist, letterer, and editor.

There is no single prescribed format for a comic script. However, there are two dominant approaches used across the industry, and within each you will find personal variations from writer to writer. The core idea is the same in all of them: clearly communicate what is *seen* and what is *said*, panel by panel.

---

## The Two Main Scripting Styles

### 1. Full Script (a.k.a. "DC Style")

The writer delivers a complete, panel-by-panel script — visual descriptions and all dialogue — before the artist begins drawing. This gives the writer maximum control over pacing and composition, and is the industry standard for working with letterers. It is the approach used at DC Comics and most modern publishers, and the one this guide focuses on in detail.

**Examples and resources:**
- [Blambot — Comic Script Basics](https://blambot.com/pages/comic-script-basics) — An annotated script page from Fred Van Lente's *Brain Boy* with a detailed breakdown of every element. Images of both the script and the finished page are included.
- [Creator Resource — Anatomy of a Comic Script](https://www.creatorresource.com/anatomy-of-a-comic-script/) — Fred Van Lente walks through his own format element by element, with images of script pages alongside the finished art.
- [Comic Book Script Archive](https://www.comicbookscriptarchive.com/archive/the-scripts/) — A large archive of full scripts from professional writers including Neil Gaiman, Brian K. Vaughan, Warren Ellis, Grant Morrison, and many others. Excellent for seeing how different writers apply the same basic format in their own voice.

---

### 2. Plot Script (a.k.a. "Marvel Method" or "Marvel Style")

The writer submits a story synopsis or plot outline. The artist then breaks it down into pages and panels entirely on their own — making pacing and composition decisions the writer would normally control. Once the art is finished, the writer returns to add captions and dialogue. This method was pioneered by Stan Lee and artists like Jack Kirby and Steve Ditko at Marvel in the 1960s.

The Marvel Method gives artists tremendous creative freedom and can produce unexpected, dynamic storytelling. The tradeoff is that the writer gives up control over pacing, and late-stage editorial changes become harder to accommodate. It is now less common at major publishers, but some writers still use it — particularly when working with a trusted, experienced artist.

**Examples and resources:**
- [Frank Gogol — How to Format a Comic Script](https://www.frankgogol.com/making-comics/how-to-format-a-comic-script-explained/) — Covers both plot and full script with examples and compares several formatting variations used by working writers today.
- [Alec Worley — How I Format a Comic Book Script](https://alecworley.substack.com/p/how-i-format-a-comic-book-script) — A 2000 AD writer explains his full script approach in depth, with a downloadable PDF example and the corresponding finished art page.

### A Note on Variations

Within these two main styles, individual writers develop their own conventions. Some number dialogue lines continuously across a page; others restart numbering in each panel. Some use shorthand panel notation (`1.1`, `1.2`) instead of writing "PANEL" each time. Some keep one script page per comic page strictly; others let descriptions spill over. None of this matters as long as it is consistent and clear to your collaborators.

The rest of this guide focuses on the **Full Script** format — the most common approach and the best starting point for writers working with an artist for the first time.

---

## Why Format Matters

Think of the artist as your director of photography. If you hand them a dense literary paragraph, they may miss a crucial visual detail — a prop, a gesture, a character's expression. The structured script format prevents that. It separates what is *seen* from what is *said*, giving the artist room to plan their composition and the letterer room to place speech balloons.

As letterer and designer Nate Piekos of [Blambot](https://blambot.com/pages/comic-script-basics) puts it: *simplicity and specificity* are the guiding principles of a well-formed script.

---

## The Basic Hierarchy

Every graphic novel script is organized in three levels:

```
ISSUE / CHAPTER
  └── PAGE
        └── PANEL
```

Each panel has two parts: a **visual description** and a **lettering area**.

---

## Script Format: Step by Step

### 1. The Page Header

Every new script page begins on a new document page. The header identifies the project and the writer.

```
TITLE OF YOUR BOOK — Issue #1
Written by [Your Name]
```

Use a large, bold page number so the entire creative team can navigate the document quickly.

---

### 2. The Page Number

State the page number clearly and prominently — bold and impossible to miss.

```
PAGE ONE
```

---

### 3. Panels

Number each panel under its page. Panel numbers should be nearly as visible as the page number.

```
PANEL 1
PANEL 2
```

---

### 4. The Visual Description (Panel Description)

After the panel number, write a concise description of what the artist should draw. Include:

- **Shot type** — wide shot, close-up, over-the-shoulder, bird's-eye view, etc.
- **Characters present** and what they are doing
- **Setting and mood** — time of day, location, atmosphere
- **Any key props** that matter to the story
- **Reference links** — the internet is your best friend; if you need a specific car model, building, or uniform, include a URL

Keep descriptions focused. The artist is a storyteller too — trust them with the visual craft. Your job is to specify *what* needs to be seen, not to illustrate every brushstroke.

```
PANEL 1

Wide establishing shot. A narrow alley in East London at night. Cracked
neon signs reflect off wet pavement. DANA (30s, leather jacket, tense)
presses her back against a dumpster, phone clutched to her chest.
```

---

### 5. The Lettering Area

Directly beneath the panel description is the lettering area — everything that will appear as text in the finished panel. Number each item sequentially. This numbering helps editors assign balloon placements to the letterer.

Each item has a **call-out** (who is speaking or what kind of text it is) followed by the text itself.

```
1. DANA: I know you're out there.
2. CAPTION (TIME/PLACE): London. 2:17 a.m.
3. SFX: KRNCH
```

**Indenting** dialogue (not tabbing) keeps the document clean for the letterer, who otherwise has to run find-and-replace operations to strip tab characters before lettering begins.

---

## Common Call-Out Types

| Call-out | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `CHARACTER:` | Standard dialogue in a speech balloon |
| `CHARACTER (OFF):` | Character is speaking from off-panel |
| `CHARACTER (WHISPER):` | Dialogue in a smaller or dashed balloon |
| `CHARACTER (BURST):` | Shouted dialogue in a jagged burst balloon |
| `CHARACTER (SINGING):` | Usually accompanied by a music note symbol |
| `CHARACTER (WEAK):` | Diminished speech — faint or dying voice |
| `CAPTION (CHARACTER):` | Inner thought or narration box for a character |
| `CAPTION (TIME/PLACE):` | Location or time stamp, e.g. "Jerusalem, 1948" |
| `CAPTION (VOICE OVER):` | Character speaking but not present in the scene |
| `NARRATION:` | Third-person narrative caption |
| `SFX:` | Sound effect |

---

## Formatting Tips for Text

- Write dialogue in **sentence case**, not ALL CAPS. The letterer will format it in the comic.
- Words that should appear **bold** in the final balloon should be bold (and/or underlined) in the script.
- Non-English dialogue should be in *italics*.
- Full blocks of dialogue translated into English should be wrapped in `< >` angle brackets, with a caption indicating the language.

## A Full Example Page

```
MY GRAPHIC NOVEL — Chapter 1
Written by Jane Doe

---

PAGE ONE

PANEL 1

Wide shot. The rooftop of an old apartment building in London. Dawn.
Grey light bleeds over the Thames. ELIAS (50s, salt-and-pepper
stubble, rumpled coat) stands at the edge, looking out at the river.
He holds an old photograph.

1. CAPTION (ELIAS): Forty years, and the city still looks the same to me.

---

PANEL 2

Close-up on the photograph in Elias's hand. It shows a young couple
— Elias in his 20s, and a woman laughing, the bay behind them.

(No dialogue.)

---

PANEL 3

Medium shot. Elias turns as he hears a sound. Behind him, a DOOR
bursts open. MIRA (late 20s, out of breath, a manila folder under
her arm) rushes onto the rooftop.

2. MIRA: They found the archive. All of it.
3. ELIAS: That's impossible.
4. MIRA (WHISPER): I know.
```

## Key Principles to Remember

**Separate the seen from the said.** The visual description tells the artist what to draw. The lettering area tells the letterer what to place. Never mix them.

**Be specific, not literary.** "A dark, oppressive room that feels like a coffin" is atmosphere. "A bare interrogation room, single hanging bulb, no windows" is an instruction. Both can coexist — lead with the instruction.

**Trust your collaborators.** The script is a communication document, not a finished product. Your artist, letterer, and editor are creative partners. Give them what they need; leave room for their craft.

**One page of script ≠ one page of comic.** A single script page (with its panels) becomes one printed page in the book. Plan your panel count accordingly — most pages have three to six panels.

**Use the industry-standard tool.** Scripts are written in MS Word (`.docx`), the industry standard. Use simple formatting that anyone can open and read.
