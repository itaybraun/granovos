# The Treatment

## Before you write a single panel, you need to sell the world.

A treatment is not a script. It is not a synopsis. It exists in a peculiar in-between space: detailed enough to convince a publisher that your story is complete and inevitable, yet open enough that the art hasn't been strangled before it breathes. Think of it as the architect's elevation drawing — the building doesn't exist yet, but you can already feel the light inside it.

The treatment is, above all, a pitch document. Its audience is not the reader of your finished book; its audience is the editor, the publisher, or the producer who needs to understand in five pages or fewer why this story must exist, why it must be a graphic novel, and why you are the one to make it.

> **Definition:** A graphic novel treatment is a concise document — typically one to five pages — that lays out the story, characters, visual world, and emotional argument of your project before the full script is written.

---

## What a treatment contains

Every treatment is different, but strong ones share the same bones.

**01 — The logline**
One or two sentences that name the protagonist, the conflict, and the stakes. If you cannot write this, the story is not yet clear enough to pitch.

**02 — The synopsis**
A full narrative summary — beginning, middle, and end. No cliffhangers. Publishers need to know where the story lands before they invest in it.

**03 — Character breakdown**
Brief portraits of your key players: who they are, what they want, what stands in their way, and a hint of how they look on the page.

**04 — World & visual style**
The atmosphere, the time and place, the palette. What does this world feel like to draw? Reference artists or describe the line quality if it helps.

**05 — Tone & theme**
What the story is about beneath the plot. The emotional or philosophical question it asks. The audience it speaks to.

**06 — Sample art** *(optional)*
If you have a collaborating artist, include two or three pages of finished or rough art. Nothing sells a visual medium like the visuals themselves.

---

## The thing most treatments forget

Film treatments describe action. Prose novel treatments describe voice and interiority. A graphic novel treatment must do something different: it must make the reader feel that this story cannot exist in any other medium.

That means your treatment should hint at the visual logic of the book. Are there pages with no dialogue — just sequential images building dread? Does the panel structure fragment when the protagonist's mind fractures? Is colour used symbolically, bleeding away as hope recedes? You don't need to script these moments in a treatment. But you need to gesture toward them, so the reader understands that you are thinking in pictures, not just in plot.

> **A useful test:** After drafting your treatment, ask: could this story work as a short film? As a prose novella? If the answer is easily yes, return to the treatment and find what is uniquely, irreducibly visual about it. The medium is not a container for your story — it is part of the story's meaning.

---

## Practical guidance

- Keep it to five pages maximum. If you cannot contain the story, you have not yet mastered it. Editors notice when a treatment sprawls.

- Write the synopsis in present tense, as if the story is unfolding now. "Mara discovers the letters" reads with more urgency than "Mara discovered the letters."

- Include the ending. It is a common mistake to withhold it for drama. Publishers are not readers; they need the full shape of the story to evaluate the project.

- Give the visual style section real specificity. "Influenced by Moebius and 1970s French SF illustration, loose ink with flat colour and heavy spot blacks" is useful. "A unique and dynamic art style" is not.

- Name your target audience, and mean it. Not "for everyone who loves great stories," but "adults 25–40 familiar with magical realism and interested in stories about grief."

- If the treatment is for an unsolicited pitch, check submission guidelines. Some publishers want treatments first; others want a complete script or first issue. Sending the wrong document signals that you haven't done your homework.

---

*The treatment is often the hardest document a graphic novelist writes — harder, in some ways, than the script itself. It asks you to know the whole story before you have lived inside it. But the discipline of writing it will clarify your project, expose its weaknesses, and make the scripting that follows considerably cleaner. Write it seriously. Revise it carefully. Then let it go and begin the work of making the book itself.*

## Example treatment: *Persepolis* by Marjane Satrapi

*What follows is a reconstructed treatment — written as Satrapi might have pitched it to her publisher, L'Association, in Paris around 1999. The actual pitch documents were never published. This example is written in the spirit and structure of a real treatment, drawn from the finished work, to show you concretely what each section looks like in practice.*

---

**Project:** Persepolis
**Author/Artist:** Marjane Satrapi
**Format:** Black-and-white graphic memoir, approx. 150 pages
**Publisher target:** L'Association (France); potential English-language rights

---

### Logline

A girl grows up in Tehran as the Islamic Revolution dismantles the world she knows — and discovers that the only thing harder than surviving a dictatorship is surviving yourself.

---

### Synopsis

The story opens in 1979. Marjane (Marji) is nine years old, the only child of a progressive, intellectual Tehran family. Her father is an engineer; her mother, a feminist activist. Her beloved uncle Anoosh is a political prisoner who has spent years in Soviet exile for his communist beliefs. Marji grows up in a home full of books, argument, and love — and outside, the Islamic Revolution is remaking Iran from the ground up.

We follow Marji through the years of transformation: the mandatory veil, the closure of her French bilingual school, the arbitrary rules that carve her childhood into before and after. Her family's politics make them suspect. As the Iran-Iraq War begins, the city fills with air raid sirens, food queues, and the gold keys that the government gives to poor children so they will walk into minefields believing paradise awaits. Uncle Anoosh is arrested again, and this time he is executed. On the night she learns of his death, Marji loses her faith in God — a God she had been having conversations with since she was small, a God who appears in her bedroom like an old friend. The panel is black. Empty. She is alone in a way she has never been before.

The story accelerates through Marji's adolescence: her rebellion, her punk cassette tapes smuggled under her coat, her Iron Maiden button worn beneath the veil. She grows sharp-tongued, impossible, wonderful. Her parents, afraid she will be arrested or worse, make the devastating decision to send her to Vienna to study when she is fourteen. The last image of Part One is the airport. Her mother faints. Her father holds her. Marji watches them through the window of the departures gate until they disappear.

Part Two follows Marji in Vienna — freezing, lonely, exhilarated, lost. She cycles through boarding houses, friendships, love affairs, and one long, terrible winter spent homeless on the streets. She returns to Tehran at twenty, thinner and broken in ways her parents cannot name. She finishes art school. She marries a man she doesn't quite love. She divorces him. And eventually, when she understands that Iran under the theocracy has no room for the woman she has become, she leaves again — this time, she knows, for good. The final image: Marji in a taxi to the airport, watching Tehran shrink behind her. She does not look away.

---

### Character breakdown

**Marjane (Marji) Satrapi** — The narrator, drawn as a child and then as a young woman. Round-faced, big-eyed, opinionated from birth. She wants to be a prophet at age six, a revolutionary at ten, and simply herself at twenty-five. On the page she is drawn with the economy of a child's sketch — simple lines, enormous expressiveness. She carries the whole weight of the book in her face.

**Ebi (father)** — Gentle, funny, politically committed. He reads Descartes and Marx. He weeps at the airport. He is the person Marji most wants to make proud, and the person she is most afraid of disappointing. His visual silhouette is round, warm, slightly rumpled.

**Tadji (mother)** — The fiercer parent. Activist, beautiful, uncompromising. She dyes her hair red. She marches in demonstrations even after it becomes dangerous. When she collapses at the airport it is the only time in the book we see her fall. She is drawn with strong vertical lines — upright, always.

**Uncle Anoosh** — Communist, dreamer, storyteller. He gives Marji a bread swan he sculpted in prison. He is executed before she is fifteen. He is the first person she truly loses, and his absence haunts the rest of the book in the space where his stories used to be.

**God** — An elderly bearded figure who sits on Marji's bedroom floor and has conversations with her about justice, revolution, and what it means to be chosen. He disappears after Anoosh's death and never returns.

---

### World and visual style

Tehran in the late 1970s and 1980s: a city layered with contradiction. Ornate Persian tilework behind new revolutionary murals. Chador-clad women carrying designer handbags. Western pop music beneath the sound of the call to prayer. The visual world of the book must hold this contradiction — the familiar made strange, the repressive made absurd.

The art is entirely black and white. No grey. This is a deliberate formal argument: in a world of official ideology, nuance is the first casualty. Black and white also honours the genre's roots — this book belongs in the tradition of Spiegelman's *Maus*, which the author read and which demonstrated that the graphic form can hold the gravest of human histories.

The line is simple, almost childlike in the early pages — Marji's visual world as she experiences it. As she ages, the line becomes more controlled, more shadowed. The book itself grows up with her. Panel composition shifts with emotional register: tight grids for the orderly oppression of the revolutionary state; full-bleed black panels for grief; loose, expanding panels for moments of freedom or joy.

Key visual motifs: the veil (worn, removed, thrown over a shoulder, pressed down by force); the window (Marji watching her parents from departures, Marji watching Tehran from a taxi); the staircase (she always seems to be arriving or leaving); and bodies in silhouette — anonymous crowds, soldiers, child soldiers, the dead.

Reference visual touchstones: Persian miniature painting (decorative borders, frontal figures), German Expressionist woodcuts (the heavy blacks, the stark contrast), and the bande dessinée tradition of Satrapi's adopted France.

### Tone and theme

This is a coming-of-age story and a political history simultaneously. The tone moves between the two without warning — a chapter about the mechanics of revolution followed by a chapter about a girl's first crush. This tonal range is not inconsistency; it is the book's central formal argument. History does not pause for childhood. Childhood does not pause for history.

The themes are identity, exile, and the cost of bearing witness. Marji's question across the whole book is: how do I remain myself when every institution — the state, the religion, the school, eventually even the family — is asking me to become someone else? The answer the book gives is partial and honest: with difficulty, with humor, with failure, and with love.

**Target audience:** Adults 25–50. Readers of literary memoir, readers interested in Middle Eastern history and women's narratives, readers who came to comics through *Maus* or *Fun Home*. The book is also widely taught in secondary schools and universities — an audience the publisher should consider in marketing.

**Comparable titles:** Art Spiegelman's *Maus* (Holocaust memoir in comics form); Joe Sacco's *Palestine* (graphic journalism); Alison Bechdel's *Fun Home* (graphic autobiography).

---

### A note on the visual medium

This story could not be told in prose. The image does work that language cannot. When Marji's uncle is executed, the book gives us a single black panel — no text, no image, nothing. The emptiness is the grief. A prose memoir would have to describe that emptiness; here, the reader inhabits it. When Marji wears her Iron Maiden pin under her veil, we see both at once — the public face and the private rebellion in the same drawing. This simultaneity is unique to the graphic form.

The book also requires the reader to hold two kinds of information at once: the child's limited understanding and the adult narrator's retrospective knowledge. Comics do this through the gap between caption and image — we read what Marji thought then and see what she couldn't see yet. This is the formal argument that makes the medium essential to the story, not merely convenient.

---

*Note to reader of this guide: What makes this treatment work is not the quality of the writing alone — though it is precise and specific throughout. What makes it work is that every section keeps returning to the question of why this is a graphic novel. The visual style section does not merely describe aesthetics; it explains why black-and-white is a moral choice. The character section doesn't just list traits; it tells you how each person looks on the page. And the closing note makes the case, concretely and with examples, for the irreducibility of the medium. That is what your treatment must do.*
